MODULE 1: HISTORY OF ASTRONOMY, THEORIES ON MOTION, AND LAWS OF MOTION

This module will help you learn how people back then could identify the shape and even the estimated circumference of the earth without advanced technology. Also, it will deepen your understanding of how astronomers contributed so much to the emergence of correct information about the solar system and the entire cosmos. Aside from the mentioned, you will get yourself informed about how Galileo corrected all of Aristotle’s assumptions about motion that led to the discovery of free-fall, vertical upward motion, and projectile motion. Finally, you will get to recall and master information about the laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation and their applications to other principles that include momentum, impulse-momentum theorem, and friction.

After going through this module, you should be able to:
  1. Explain how the Greeks knew that the earth is spherical.
  2. Cite examples of astronomical phenomena known to astronomers before the advent of the telescope.
  3. Explain how Brahe’s innovations and extensive data collection in observational astronomy paved the way for Kepler’s discovery of his laws of planetary motion.
  4. Comparing and contrasting the Aristotelian and Galilean Conceptions of vertical motion, horizontal motion, and projectile motion.
  5. Explain how Galileo inferred that objects in a vacuum fall with uniform acceleration and that force are not necessary to sustain horizontal motion.
  6. Perform calculations about free-falling bodies.
  7. Explaining the subtle distinction between Newton’s 1st Law of Motion (or Law of Inertia) and Galileo’s assertion that force is not necessary to sustain horizontal motion.
  8. Use algebra to calculate problems involving Newton’s 2nd Law of Motion and Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation.
  9. Use the law of conservation of momentum to solve one-dimensional collision problems.
